Who we mean by “village”

Scale · geography · human development · why metering choices matter now

IEEE Smart Village works on village-scale energy — communities too small for utility-style advanced metering, too large for a single solar home system, and often growing faster than the grid can reach them. This page defines that scope in plain language and cites independent sources for population and access numbers. For operational problems and solutions, see Problems to solve.

How ISV defines a village (working scope)

There is no single UN legal definition of “village.” National statistics use different cutoffs for rural vs urban. For engineering and procurement on this wiki, ISV uses an operational definition:

  • Settlement scale — a localized community or cluster, often tens to a few thousand people, served by one or a few low-voltage feeders rather than a metropolitan grid.
  • Utility model — a mini-grid, micro-utility, or community distributor that sells electricity per connection (often prepaid), not a national transmission utility.
  • Connection band — roughly 50–500 customer connections per feeder is the sweet spot for Village Metering work: large enough to need per-home billing and theft control, small enough that city-scale MDMS and certification are overkill. [ISV working band — validate per deployment]
  • Geography — primarily rural and peri-urban areas in Africa and Asia where ISV field partners deploy; includes remote indigenous communities (e.g. Alaska field notes) where the same metering economics apply.
  • Not in scope — metropolitan AMI rollouts, national utility head-ends, or single-home pico-solar unless they feed a broader feeder study.

Industry language overlaps: mini-grid, micro-grid, decentralized utility, and energy access enterprise often describe the same operator class. World Bank and AMDA sector work treats mini-grids as localized distribution serving rural and peri-urban customers where the main grid is absent or unreliable [s5] [s6].

Rural population is still growing in absolute terms in many regions even as the world urbanizes — so “wait for the grid” is not a passive default. UN World Urbanization Prospects tracks rural/urban splits by national definitions [s3].

Electricity access is a separate measure: a household may be rural but electrified (grid, mini-grid, or solar home system), or urban but without reliable supply. Tracking SDG 7 reports global access stagnating around 92% in 2024, with progress too slow to meet the 2030 universal-access goal without a sharp acceleration [s1].

Sub-Saharan Africa accounts for the majority of people still in the dark; IEA analysis notes roughly eight in ten people globally without electricity live there [s2]. Electrification has often not kept pace with population growth — the rural deficit in SSA grew over the last decade even as some countries expanded connections [s1].

South Asia and other regions also host large unelectrified populations, but SSA combines low access rates with high rural share (~60% rural population in many analyses) and young demographics — so demand per connected customer rises as villages mechanize [s7].

Mini-grids and village utilities — scale of the opportunity

For communities the main grid will not serve soon, decentralized mini-grids are a primary connection path alongside grid extension and solar home systems. Estimates vary by scenario:

  • World Bank / ESMAP analysis: mini-grids could connect on the order of 490 million people by 2030, requiring hundreds of thousands of sites and major investment — but with improving uptime vs earlier diesel-era systems [s5].
  • Sector surveys: thousands of mini-grids already operate in SSA; private developers report hundreds of thousands of rural customers on decentralized utilities [s6].
  • Literature reviews cite ~11 million mini-grid-related connections in SSA in recent years and project large household impact by 2030 if policy and finance align [s7].

ISV does not endorse one global headcount — we cite ranges and focus on operators ISV can help: village-scale utilities with prepaid billing, mixed meter fleets, and limited O&M staff.

Human development — why energy is not a side issue

The UN Human Development Index (HDI) summarizes health, education, and income. Electricity is not a direct HDI input, but it underpins all three: clinics, schools, productive enterprise, and safe cooking/lighting [s8].

The Multidimensional Poverty Index (MPI) — published with the Human Development Report — explicitly counts lack of electricity as a deprivation indicator (aligned with SDG 7). Recent MPI updates report about 1.1 billion people in acute multidimensional poverty; missing electricity is among the most common living-standard deprivations alongside cooking fuel, sanitation, and housing [s9].

UNDP satellite-based work argues official “electrified” counts can overstate reliable access — settlements with weak or intermittent supply may still fail productive use. That gap matters for metering: a connection on paper is not the same as trustworthy kWh accounting [s10].

Rapid growth — modernization fork

Villages in low-access regions are not static. Population growth, diaspora remittances, motor loads, cold chains, and phone-first payments change feeders faster than utility procurement cycles. There is a real choice between:

Healthier modernization (what Village Metering supports)
  • Open, documented interfaces — operators can swap meter brands, integrate mobile money, and migrate vendors without rewriting billing.
  • Edge-first resilience — prepaid tokens, keypads, and pole gateways keep working when backhaul fails; cloud is sync, not single point of failure.
  • Right-sized investment — per-connection costs in cited industry bands (~$40–110 installed smart meter range in mini-grid literature) instead of utility AMI budgets [s11].
  • Productive anchors — schools, clinics, mills, or businesses that stabilize revenue (ISV field pattern; see Circles of Power notes).
Unhealthy modernization (patterns we document to avoid)
  • City AMI pasted onto villages — full MDMS, 15-minute load profiles, and per-meter WAN crypto where a pole gateway would suffice.
  • Closed cloud bundles — one vendor owns mesh, meters, and billing; migration strands installed base (documented in ISV Dev Labs downtime analysis) [s12].
  • Cloud-synchronous control — disconnect, vending, or theft response that fails when rural GPRS drops; multi-day outages without local fallbacks.
  • Billing without visibility — flat tariffs or aggregate feeders that hide theft until the operator is insolvent.

Metering sits at the center of that fork: it is how a growing village utility stays solvency-positive while adopting solar, storage, and income-generating loads. That is why IEEE Smart Village Tech Comm treats Village Metering as a primary endeavor — not meter worship, but governance infrastructure for humane electrification.
